How religious lobbying influences EU the law

Religious organisations spent tens of millions of euros in the last decade to shape European Union policy and law making, according to an analysis of datasets and reports by the European Commission and Parliament.

Since the creation of the EU, these institutions have invested money and time to advise members of the European Parliament and the European Commission on key topics, in order to influence law.

In the last three years, thousands of religious and non-religious bodies together spent 2 billion euros on lobbying in the European Union, according to the EU Transparency Register.

It is legal for Churches and their affiliated organizations to spend money on lobbying. They aim to address a variety of issues, including contentious legal practices like abortion and same-sex marriage.

Research on religious lobbying by Dr. Lucian N. Leustean, a professor at Aston University, showed that there is ‘a widespread perception that religious issues have been absent from the process of European integration’.

However, religious organisations have been present at EU level since its creation. The 2007 Lisbon Treaty made the unofficial official, allowing religious entities to try to influence the law. and that is exactly what they are doing.

In 2012, pro-life organisations, including the One of Us campaign and ADF International, opposed a proposal for safer legal abortion, and other issues affecting women’s sexual rights.Due to their efforts, an alternative law was passed that missed the main health concern of the initiative. Besides, this law reaffirmed that religious issues remain under the jurisdiction of EU national member states.

In the last three years, religious organizations spent 18 million euros on lobbying in the EU, but the organizations’ expenditure could be even higher, according to Gianluca Sgueo, a research analyst at the European Parliament.

“Transparency International, and another organization called Corporate Europe Observatory, they do checks on the register from time to time, and more often than not they realize the numbers are not there in an appropriate way.”

The Church of Sweden spent more than 13 million on lobbying the EU. This is almost three times more than all other religious organisations put together. When Dr Lucian N. Leustean was questioned about this disproportionate amount, he explained he would make a connection between national legislation of church-state relations in Sweden and developments at EU level on religious dialogue’.

Nonetheless, lobbying is not only about money. Meetings with EU officials are crucial for lobbyists to shape EU policies. Leustean’s research reveals that the number of official meetings between EU officials and religious institutions increased between 1982 and 2008 with Catholic and Protestant churches dominant.

Over the last three years, the EKD (Evanlegical church in Germany) declared 9 meetings with EU officials and the COMECE (Commission of the Bishops' Conferences of the European Community) declared 5. This is more than twice as many for any other registered religious organisations.

Most of these registered groups are Christian. In fact, 95% of the total amount of money spent on religious lobbying these last three years came from Christian institutions whereas Christians represent only 76% of the European population. They are dominating the religious lobbying scene.

Christian organisations account for 95% of the religious lobbying expenditures

The Transparency Register has 11,692 European and international bodies registered as active lobbyists. 51 are recorded as religious institutions and they spent 0.9% of the total amount of lobbying expenditures.

The register is voluntary, although those who participate gain greater access to higher level figures. It has been criticised for containing inaccurate information, and although there are proposals to make it mandatory, calls for a compulsory register have existed as long as it has, and none have come to pass.

However, the real number of groups and the amount of money they spent representing particular religious interests are speculated to be much larger.

According to the ‘Study for policy makers on opposition to sexual and reproductive health and rights in Europe’ by Elena Zacharenko, many organizations listed as NGOs and Think Tanks are in fact defending religious values.

Religious organisations are registered under different sections

Gianluca Sgeuo highlights an issue with EU lobbying policy, explaining: ‘The issue is not with the transparency register. It is with what the EU defines as an NGO’ He added ‘a body that has some duties, some tasks in the religious spaces can register legally as an NGO’ because of a lack of regulation.

Zacheronko’s report identifies 19 organisations that aim to influence EU law to promote values, either through lobbying politicians or campaigning to change the opinion of EU citizens. However, 10 of them are not registered as religious organisations in the Transparency Register. When asked to comment, both The Institute of Bioethics and HazteOir claimed not to represent any religion.

The Institute of Bioethics denied any involvement in lobbying activities. “We don’t do lobbying but information, we are journalists” claimed Carine Brochier, the Institute’s project manager. HazteOir responded: ‘HazteOir.org and CitizenGO, as non-religious associations with more than 9 million active citizens: atheists, and believers of different religions, all over the world; we defend life, family, and several freedoms: of speech, religious, of education’.

Despite these denials, HazteOir and CitizenGO work alongside explicitly religious groups, such as FAFCE (Federation of Catholic Family Associations in Europe), with whom they co-signed a letter protesting a directive “implementing the principle of equal treatment between persons irrespective of religion or belief, disability, age or sexual orientation”. The Institute of Bioethics’ Honour Committee includes Miroslav Mikolasik and Anna Zaborska, of the both involved in the slovak “Christian Democratic Movement" party.

The main religious lobbying campaigns since 2010

The professionalization of religious lobbying coincides with their participation in new debates as the climate change or immigration. But Christian lobbyists’ main fight is and will likely remain what we call the Sexual and Reproductive Health and Right (SRHR). This includes abortion, families right, same-sex marriage… When it comes to these topics, they often unify to try to influence the EU law. Indeed, since 2010, they had some significant victories.

A, B, C V IRELAND

Goal: Be allowed to travel to the UK to be aborted because abortions are unlawful in Ireland

Who was involved: three women against the Irish law

methods used: Go in front of European Court of Human Right

ONE OF US CAMPAIGN

Goal: stop EU funding for any activities involving the destruction of the human embryo 

Who was involved: One of Us Federation - COMECE (Conferences of the European Community) - ECPM (European Christian Political Movement)

methods used: Inform and mobilize the public opinion thanks to active social media campaigns

EDITE ESTRELA REPORT

Goal: Block a pro-choice report promoting safer abortion and xomen's rights

Who was involved: European Dignity Watch - FAFCE

methods used: mass emailing, battle of information, social media campaigns

#DEFUNDIPPF

Goal: Defund IPPF organisation

Who was involved: One of Us - European Diginty Watch - ADF International

methods used: Social media campaigns, battle of information

Most of these campaigns were successful for the religious campaigners either by their importance or their result.

The Edite Estrela report aimed to pass a law facilitating abortion across all EU because it’s still difficult or even illegal to obtain abortion in many countries, such as as Ireland or Poland. But the European Parliament decided that such decisions should be made by member states at a national level.

The ‘One of Us’ campaign started as a European Citizens’ Initiative, but was quickly backed by churches. They launched a petition to stop any EU funding for projects that included destroying human embryos, which gathered over 1.5 million signatures online. The European Commission rejected this proposal, causing the campaign groups to bring their case to the European Court of Justice.

The A, B, C v Ireland was a victory for Ireland. The European Court of Justice considered that Ireland constitution doesn’t confer a right to abortion except when it could be life-threatening for the future mother.

#DefundIPPF didn’t lead to the expected result of defunding this organisation, nonetheless, it gave the opportunity to religious lobbyists, affiliated organisations and MEPs to unify and campaign together to try to interfere.

These campaigns, successful or not are strong example of the power of lobbyists when they unify.

Increasingly, lobbyists use such social media campaigns to influence law indirectly. Gianluca Sgueo described this as an ‘unofficial’ way to lobby, ‘to basically put the public opinion against the decision from the institutions’. Successful or not, these campaigns demonstrate the impact lobbyists are capable of when unified.

The network of religious lobbyists

These campaigns bring together different campaigning groups, and highlight the connections between them. Dr. Leustean’s research sees a growing trend of professionalisation in religious lobbying, and he claims the “last ten years have been characterised by a change from proactive relations to the institutionalisation of religious dialogue in the EU.”

Gianluca Sgueo broardens this, arguing that “one of the trends I think everyone agrees, that has developed over the last 10 years is the fact that, NGOs, specifically, is much more organised in terms of skills to lobby the EU institutions”.

This has resulted in strong relationships between influential individuals, including MEPs, lobbying organisations and religious groups, with key individuals linking many groups. For instance, former Italian MEP Luca Volonte is CEO and founder of Nova Terrae Foundation, Chairman of the Dignitatis Humanae Institute, and Advisor to the International Centre for Law, Family and Life (ICOLF). In 2016, Volente allegedly accepted approximately €2.4 million from Azerbaijani officials in exchange for “his support of political positions of the state” at the Council.

Nova Terrae Foundation’s network includes Ireland’s ‘The Iona Institute’, a socially conservative Catholic advocacy group,. The Foundation is also partners with ECLJ, Hatze Oir, The Heritage Foundation, Fondation Jerome Lejeune, and organises petitions with CitizenGo.
